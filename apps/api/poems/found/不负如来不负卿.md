# 不负如来不负卿

_author: 仓央嘉措
_date: 1700


美人不是母胎生，应是桃花树长成，
已恨桃花容易落，落花比汝尚多情。

静时修止动修观，历历情人挂目前，
若将此心以学道，即生成佛有何难？

结尽同心缔尽缘，此生虽短意缠绵，
与卿再世相逢日，玉树临风一少年。

不观生灭与无常，但逐轮回向死亡，
绝顶聪明矜世智，叹他于此总茫茫。

山头野马性难驯，机陷犹堪制彼身，
自叹神通空具足，不能调伏枕边人。

欲倚绿窗伴卿卿，颇悔今生误道行，
有心持钵丛林去，又负美人一片情。

静坐修观法眼开，祈求三宝降灵台，
观中诸圣何曾见？不请情人却自来。

入山投谒得道僧，求教上师说因明，
争奈相思无拘检，意马心猿到卿卿。

曾虑多情损梵行，入山又恐别倾城，
世间安得双全法，不负如来不负卿。


The lovely one was never born of womb;
she flowered from a peach tree’s tender bloom.
I mourn how easily those petals fall—
they love you deeper than my vows recall.

In stillness I school breath; in motion, sight;
yet, clear as stars, your face returns each night.
If this same heart could turn to seek the Way,
what hindrance then to Buddhahood today?

We knotted soul to soul with threads unseen;
though brief this life, our meanings twine between.
When next we meet across another year,
I’ll come—a wind-touched youth, unshadowed, clear.

I do not count the births, the deaths, the change;
I chase the wheel that wanders through its range.
The proud, most clever, vaunt their worldly art—
and lose themselves upon the dust-blown heart.

Wild on the ridge, the mountain stallion runs;
a snare might hold his hooves, but never suns.
Alas—my vaunted powers prove untrue:
I tame all else, yet cannot master you.

I’d lean by your green lattice, close and mild,
and grieve the vows that turned my feet from wild.
To roam with begging bowl among the pines—
I wrong your faithful heart with monkish lines.

In silent watch my dharma-eyes arise;
I beg the Triple Jewel to grace my skies.
No saint appears to answer what I cry—
unbidden, you return and stand nearby.

I climbed to greet a sage in mountain air;
he taught me cause and fruit, the karmic snare.
But law cannot restrain what longing knew—
my mind’s wild horse runs straight and sure to you.

I feared that love would stain the cloistered vow;
I fear more still to lose your nearness now.
Where in this world is any perfect way—
to keep the Buddha, and not let you stray?


——————


The beauty was not born of a mother’s womb;
she must have grown from a peach-blossom tree.
I already mourn how easily peach flowers fall;
fallen petals are still more affectionate than you.

In stillness I practice śamatha; in movement, vipaśyanā—
yet my beloved appears distinctly before my eyes.
If this very heart were turned to the Path,
how hard would Buddhahood be in this very life?

We tied our hearts and sealed our karmic bonds;
though brief this life, our affections stay entwined.
On the day I meet you again in a later life,
I shall be a youth, a jade tree in the wind.

I do not contemplate arising, ceasing, and impermanence;
I only pursue samsara toward its deathward course.
The supremely clever, proud of worldly wit,
sigh—here they are altogether at a loss.

On the ridge the wild horse is hard to tame;
snares and devices can yet restrain its body.
I lament my “powers” are an empty having—
I cannot subdue the one beside my pillow.

I would lean by the green lattice to keep you company;
I much regret that in this life I have missed the Way.
I think to take the alms-bowl to the monastic groves,
yet fail the beauty’s whole-hearted love.

Sitting in quiet, cultivating insight, the dharma-eye opens;
I pray the Three Jewels to descend upon the mind’s altar.
Never once, within contemplation, are the saints seen;
unbidden, my beloved comes of herself.

I entered the mountains to visit an enlightened monk;
I asked the guru to teach Hetuvidyā.
But longing admits no check or rule—
the mind’s monkey and intention’s horse run straight to you.

I once feared deep feeling would harm brahmacarya;
entering the mountains, I feared to part from the lovely one.
How in this world can there be a way to have both—
to fail neither the Tathāgata nor you?
